The Problem: Technology Isn't the Bottleneck

Autonomous vehicles can already navigate complex road environments. The harder problem is whether humans will ever trust them enough to share the road comfortably. We are entering an era of mixed traffic — where human-driven cars and autonomous vehicles coexist on the same roads — and the quality of that coexistence depends on how predictable and legible AVs feel to the humans around them.

Lane changes are one of the most tension-filled moments in this interaction. When a vehicle cuts into your lane, you make a split-second decision about how to respond — slow down, hold speed, brake hard. Human drivers do this intuitively, reading dozens of signals at once. An AV following a rigid rule-based threshold will respond differently, and that unpredictability erodes trust over time.

TNO's Helmond department was studying exactly this: can we model how human drivers actually respond to lane-changing events, so that AVs can learn to behave in a way that feels natural rather than mechanical? My thesis sat inside this broader research question.

My Research Questions

I narrowed the problem to two tractable questions. First: does the physical situation at the moment a vehicle begins a lane change — the gap between cars, their relative speed — actually predict how strongly the approaching driver reacts? And second: which combination of parameters best helps a model forecast that velocity response?

RQ1: How does the initial relative gap and relative velocity between the approaching and lane-changing vehicle impact the velocity response?

RQ2: Which parameters best enhance the accuracy of predictive models simulating human drivers' velocity response to a lane-changing vehicle?

The assumption going in was that a larger gap or slower relative speed would produce a calmer response. What I found was more interesting — and more honest about how complicated humans are.

Choosing the Right Dataset

Before writing any analysis code, I had to find the right data. I evaluated several large-scale real-world driving datasets — including LiDAR-based collections and bird's-eye-view datasets — assessing each for scenario coverage, label quality, and the granularity of vehicle state information I would need. I chose the Waymo Open Dataset.

Waymo provides 10Hz-sampled 3D object tracks for every road agent in the scene: bounding boxes with precise centre points and headings, velocity vectors, and corresponding high-definition 3D maps including lane geometry, road edges, and traffic signal states. The motion scenarios are 9.1-second windows (91 steps at 10Hz) mined from real urban driving interactions — rich enough to study the before, during, and after of a lane-change event in detail.

Building the Lane-Change Detection Algorithm

There was no off-the-shelf solution for extracting lane-change events from Waymo data. I built the detection algorithm from scratch. The process had three stages.

Frame transformation. Waymo provides velocity in global (world) coordinates. To measure lateral motion meaningfully, I needed velocities relative to each vehicle's own direction of travel. I applied a rotation matrix using each agent's heading angle to transform global velocity components into vehicle-local coordinates — forward and lateral separately. Without this step, a vehicle turning on a curve would produce misleading lateral velocity readings.

Lane-change detection. Using Waymo's static map features — the polyline representations of lane boundaries sampled at 0.5m resolution — I detected when a vehicle's trajectory crossed a lane boundary. The algorithm identifies the lane-changing vehicle, the approaching vehicle already occupying the target lane, and the moment of intrusion.

Scenario alignment. Extracted scenarios were then filtered and time-aligned around the intrusion point so that the analysis window captures a consistent before-and-after period for every event.

Analysis & Findings

With a clean dataset of extracted scenarios, I ran two analyses.

For RQ1, I used Interrupted Time Series Analysis — a method suited to studying the effect of a discrete event on a continuous time series. The lane intrusion is the "interruption"; I was measuring whether and how strongly it changed the approaching vehicle's velocity profile. The results confirmed that the intrusion does have a measurable effect. But when I plotted the post-intrusion velocity change against the initial gap and relative velocity, there was no clean linear relationship. The scatter plots were dispersed. Some clustering appeared around certain velocity ranges, but nothing predictable enough to reduce to a threshold rule.

For RQ2, I built an ARIMAX forecasting model — an autoregressive model that incorporates exogenous variables — to test which parameter combinations best predict the approaching vehicle's velocity response. The sensitivity analysis showed that including initial conditions (gap and relative velocity at the moment of intrusion) does improve predictive accuracy. The combination of initial gap, lane-changing vehicle velocity, and initial relative velocity achieved the lowest average error. But the differences between the top-performing combinations were small enough that no single model clearly dominated.

Human velocity responses to lane changes are measurably affected by the intrusion event — but the initial physical conditions at the moment of intrusion do not predict the intensity of that response through any clean linear rule. Human behaviour is more context-dependent than current ALKS safety thresholds assume.

What I Took Away

Two things have stayed with me from this project. The first is how much domain knowledge a software engineer needs before writing a single line of code. I spent weeks understanding vehicle dynamics, coordinate systems, and the physics of lane-changing behaviour before the dataset would even make sense. That groundwork isn't optional — it's where the real decisions happen.

The second is something broader: how important it is for engineers to grapple with cultural context before releasing technology into the world. The whole premise of this research was that a technically functioning AV isn't enough. If it doesn't behave in a way that feels legible and trustworthy to human drivers, it won't be accepted — and an AV that isn't accepted can't do any good. That framing shapes how I think about any software that touches the real world.
